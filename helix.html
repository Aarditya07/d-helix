01 — OVERVIEW

The Double Helix

DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid) is the hereditary material in humans and almost all other organisms. Its iconic double helix structure was first described by Watson and Crick in 1953, building on Rosalind Franklin's X-ray crystallography data.

  • Two complementary strands wound around each other
  • Right-handed helical structure (B-DNA form)
  • Approximately 3.4 Å (Ångströms) rise per base pair
  • One full turn every 10.5 base pairs

02 — STRUCTURE

Sugar-Phosphate Backbone

The structural framework of DNA consists of alternating deoxyribose sugar and phosphate groups. This backbone forms the outer "rails" of the DNA ladder and runs in opposite directions on each strand — a property called antiparallel orientation.

  • Phosphate groups (PO₄³⁻) carry negative charges
  • Deoxyribose sugar lacks the 2′-OH group found in RNA
  • Linked together by phosphodiester bonds
  • Strand A runs 5′→3′, Strand B runs 3′→5′

03 — NITROGENOUS BASES

The Four Bases

The four nitrogenous bases store the genetic code. They pair specifically across the two strands, forming the "rungs" of the DNA ladder. Purines (two rings) always pair with Pyrimidines (one ring).

04 — HYDROGEN BONDS

Hydrogen Bonds

The two strands are held together by hydrogen bonds (shown as H··· on the 3D model) between complementary base pairs. Though individually weak, the sheer number of bonds across millions of base pairs gives DNA remarkable stability.

05 — BASE PAIRING RULES

Chargaff's Rules

Erwin Chargaff discovered that in any DNA sample, the amount of Adenine equals Thymine, and Guanine equals Cytosine. This complementarity is the foundation of DNA replication fidelity and ensures the genetic code is faithfully copied.

06 — THE CODE OF LIFE

Blueprint of Life

This elegant double helix contains all the instructions needed to build and maintain a living organism. From DNA replication to transcription and translation, every biological process begins here.

  • DNA → mRNA (Transcription by RNA Polymerase)
  • mRNA → Protein (Translation by Ribosomes)
  • DNA replication ensures genetic continuity
  • Mutations in DNA can cause disease or drive evolution
